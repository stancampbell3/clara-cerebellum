  The Shared Structural Problem

  Every method in the article shares a root limitation: they reconstruct        
  explanations for decisions that were never made explainably. The model's
  actual computation is opaque; these techniques produce an artifact that       
  approximates or correlates with it after the fact. The explanation is separate
   from the reasoning — it is a shadow of it.

  The article's complete absence of neurosymbolic, rule-based, or formal logic  
  approaches reflects where the field currently sits: post-hoc interpretation is
   the dominant paradigm because the dominant models (deep neural networks,     
  transformers) offer no native reasoning trace.            

  How Clara Differs — and What to Say in the One-Pager                          
   
  The contrast with Clara is structural, not incremental:                       
                                                            
  - Post-hoc methods explain after the fact. Clara's reasoning tableau is       
  generated as inference occurs — the explanation is a direct record of the
  computation, not a reconstruction of it.                                      
  - Post-hoc methods approximate. LIME explicitly admits it does not show what
  the model did. Clara's tableau is deterministic — same rules, same inputs,    
  same trace, every time.
  - Post-hoc methods are computationally additive. SHAP adds hours of compute on
   top of an already-trained model. Clara's explanation is a byproduct of       
  inference with no separate cost.
  - Attention is correlational, not causal. Clara's rules fire explicitly and   
  leave named entries in the tableau — there is no ambiguity about what drove a 
  result.
  - Influence functions explain data provenance. Clara explains reasoning —     
  which axioms applied, in what order, with what bindings, and where uncertainty
   was acknowledged.
